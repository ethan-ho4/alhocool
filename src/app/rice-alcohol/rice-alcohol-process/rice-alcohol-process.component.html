SAKE

Sake, the pride of Japan, is a meticulous blend of art and science. The rice, specifically sake rice, is polished to remove the bran, sometimes up to 50% of its original size! After steaming, the rice meets koji mold, which is cultivated in dedicated koji rooms. This mold not only converts starch to sugar but also imparts unique flavors. Multiple parallel fermentations occur in large tanks, where yeast transforms sugar into alcohol while koji continues its saccharification. The moromi (mash) is then pressed, and the liquid is separated from the rice solids. Some sakes even undergo pasteurization twice! SOJU

Soju, Korea's iconic spirit, often starts with a base of rice, barley, or sweet potatoes. Modern Soju production uses continuous column distillation, ensuring a high alcohol content. The spirit is then diluted with water, and sometimes, sweeteners, flavorings, or even herbs are added. Traditional Soju, however, is made using single distillation, retaining more flavors and producing a smoother texture. MIJIU

Mijiu, China's sweet nectar, often uses glutinous rice. The 'qu' used in fermentation is unique, as it contains both molds and yeasts, facilitating simultaneous saccharification and fermentation. This process can occur in various vessels, from ceramic jars to wooden vats. LAO LAO

Lao Lao, Laos' rustic charm, is made in villages across the country. The sticky rice is first steamed in bamboo containers over wood fires. After cooling, it's mixed with yeast and left to ferment in earthen jars. Distillation is often done in homemade stills, capturing the strong spirit while leaving behind the rice solids. TAPUY

Tapuy, hailing from the Philippines, is a celebration of simplicity. The glutinous rice is first cooked, then mixed with 'bubod', a traditional fermentation starter. This mixture is wrapped in leaves, often banana or palm, and left to ferment. The result is a mildly alcoholic paste.
